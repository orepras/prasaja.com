## The Danger of Becoming Unimpressed

One of the quiet tragedies of growing older is becoming "unimpressed" by things that once amazed us. The way the city lights used to feel magical. The way a new job used to spark daydreams. The way buildings used to tower above us instead of simply "existing."

We say we've matured, but sometimes we've just numbed ourselves.

We've trained our senses to filter out anything "unnecessary", _which unfortunately includes the small miracles that make life feel full_. We bury ourselves in work, that 'tk tk tk' in Slack pings, WhatsApp notifs, in deliverables, in the illusion that staying busy equals being valuable. Or maybe when we sit on airplanes and never look out the window (Oh I miss holiday flights). We walk past skyscrapers without acknowledging their height. We hear a piece of music and treat it like background noise.

Somewhere along the way, noticing becomes a luxury.  
And wonder becomes a stranger.

But I've seen what happens when life forces you into rooms where you'd trade anything just to experience something ordinary again. A walk in the sun. A good cup of coffee. The sight of your daughter laughing. A Monday morning that doesn't feel like a burden. Those moments change you. They sharpen something in you that you didn't realize had gone dull.

Coming back to work now, I don't want to simply "return to normal." I want to _return to awe_.

## Returning to Work with New Eyes

Uhh.. it's just an expression, I'm not actually buying a new eyes, 

People assume returning to work after a turbulent chapter means restarting where you left off. But in reality, you come back carrying a different set of lenses. You see things you didn't see before. You question things you previously accepted. You soften in ways you didn't think you could.

Entering an office now is *a professional ritual and beyond*; it's a small miracle of normalcy. Being able to focus on tasks again feels like a privilege. Sitting in meetings doesn't automatically drain me; sometimes it even reminds me of how alive the world is, and how many lives intersect to make a single product, system, or line of copy work.

Maybe that's the real lesson?
Awe grows when you realize how interconnected everything is.

I notice people more. The way the security guard greets everyone with the same calm energy every morning (except that one who always give me side-eye? why?). The way designers argue passionately about pixels because they care about the experience someone else will have. The way engineers explain constraints like they're translating logic into human language.

It's easy to get cynical. It's easy to say "it's just work." But you can also choose to see it differently as a collection of humans trying their best to build something useful.

And that, too, is wonder.

## The Fleeting Nature of Moments

The fact that moments are fleeting is the whole point.

If everything lasted forever, nothing would feel meaningful. The skyscraper looks different every time the sun moves. The mood of the city shifts from morning to evening. Your daughter grows, your routines change, your capacity stretches and shrinks. You only walk this version of your life once.

So for me, returning to awe is partly a rebellion. A refusal to let adulthood flatten my senses. A conscious act of tuning into the frequency of the world around me. *A disciplined softness, if that makes sense*.

Choosing to look up, even when everyone else keeps their eyes on the ground. To be moved, even at the risk of looking sentimental. To be present, even though presence makes you realize how fragile everything is.

And honestly, that fragility makes the world more beautiful.
