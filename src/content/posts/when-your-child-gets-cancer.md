At the time I publish this, _today_, is World Cancer Day, and I'm writing this from a place I never imagined I'd be—as a father learning to navigate a world where my daughter battles [rhabdomyosarcoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhabdomyosarcoma).

I still remember the before. Before the diagnosis, before the medical terminology became me and my wife's second language, before we knew what it meant to truly worry. Life was the usual chaos of finding clients and writing all-day, managing schedules, the normal complaints about being tired. We had no idea what tired actually meant.

Tired is staying up researching treatment protocols after a full day of work. It's juggling conference calls while your mind is calculating hospital visit schedules. It's the exhaustion that settles into your bones when you realize _this is something else entirely_, something you don't have a map for.

But we're not alone.

My wife—she's the kind of partner I didn't know I needed until everything fell apart. She works full-time, and shows up every single day for our daughter with this fierce, quiet strength. She's already three steps ahead of everything about planning, while I try to hold things together. We tag-team this nightmare, and I'm more grateful for her than I have words for. The recognition that you're genuinely partnered in something this hard, it changes you.

The healthcare system, though? That's its own beast. Nobody tells you how much of cancer treatment is actually about paperwork, insurance approvals, conflicting appointment times, and trying to get two different doctors' offices to communicate with each other (figuring out how oncologist, anaesthesiologist, and ENT at the same time is our breaking point). You become a project manager for your child's life, coordinating between specialists who rarely talk to each other, figuring out which ER to go to at 12 AM when something feels wrong, splitting the silence of the night with worry that your child's cancer is getting worse 

It's absurd, honestly. Your kid is sick, truly sick, and you're spending hours on hold trying to get a prior authorization approved. You're both working full-time because, well, bills don't stop for cancer, and the medical ones certainly don't. The system isn't built for this—for families who are already drowning trying to keep their heads above water.

But then there are the people. The ones who saw us struggling and just showed up.

Our families, who rearranged their lives without us even asking. Colleagues who covered for us when we needed to leave work suddenly, who stopped asking "how are you" because they knew that question had become impossible to answer. Friends who texted not with "let me know if you need anything" but with "let's meet at the weekend, you know you need this."

These are the people who check on our sanity, who remember that we're still human beings underneath the parent-of-a-sick-child identity we've had to assume. They ask about us, not just our daughter (it's fine honestly, she's kicking!). They remind us that our own mental health matters, even when we've forgotten that completely.

I'm still figuring this out—all of it. How to be present for my daughter without losing myself. How to work while also being there for treatment, every hard day, every moment she needs me. How to support my wife when I'm barely holding it together myself. How to accept help when _asking for it feels like admitting defeat_.

Some days I think I'm getting the hang of it.  
Most days I know I'm not.

What I wish, more than anything, is that information about childhood cancers like rhabdomyosarcoma was easier to find. That navigating treatment options didn't require a medical degree and a law degree simultaneously. That there were clearer paths for families trying to balance work, care, and just existing. That the system was designed for people in crisis, not against them.

I want other parents to have access to real information (not just medical facts, but the practical stuff!).

How to talk to your employer.

How to fight insurance denials.

How to maintain your marriage when everything is on fire.

How to help siblings who feel invisible.

How to take care of yourself when that feels selfish.

How to explain to your sick child why this is happening without falling apart in front of them.

Why I write this today? Because I have answers? Of course not, because there's no simple answer navigating this. Because.. maybe sharing this mess helps someone else feel less alone in their own. Because maybe if enough of us talk about it, the information will become more accessible. The support systems will get better. The healthcare maze will be less cruel.

Nada, my daughter is fighting cancer, and we're fighting alongside her. Some days we win small victories like when she now taking a pose like a model??? Some days we barely survive like last month when the chemo that needs 2 days care turns into a full week, what the fuk. But we keep showing up, together, with help from people who care.

And on World Cancer Day, I guess.. that's what I want to say? to anyone else in this, you're not alone (and if you're ever feel lonely, it's okay but don't take it a long time). To everyone who's helped us, thank you, more than you'll ever know. And to anyone with the power to make this journey easier for families, please, please do.

We're all just trying to figure this out, one day at a time.

To the families navigating childhood cancer. I see you, we see you. To the partners holding it together, you're remarkable. To the friends and family showing up, you're saving lives in ways you don't realize.

With love and model poses.
to Nada.
